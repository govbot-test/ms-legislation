SC 524 (As Introduced) - 2026 Regular Session MISSISSIPPI LEGISLATURE 2026 Regular Session To: Rules By: Senator(s) Pope, Butler, Jackson, Simmons (13th), Blount, DuPree, McLendon, Michel, Mumford, Simmons (12th), Thomas Senate Concurrent Resolution 524 A CONCURRENT RESOLUTION COMMEMORATING AND REMEMBERING THE LEGACY AND CULTURAL IMPACT OF WILLIE "THE SATELLITE" TOTTEN AND JERRY "WORLD" RICE WHO PLAYED FOR THE MISSISSIPPI VALLEY STATE DELTA DEVILS FROM 1981-1985 AND WERE NICKNAMED THE "SATELLITE EXPRESS." WHEREAS, the "Satellite Express" was the nickname for the innovative, pass-heavy offensive system and star quarterback-wide receiver duo of Willie Totten and Jerry Rice at Mississippi Valley State University (MVSU) during the early to mid-1980s; and WHEREAS, under Head Coach Archie Cooley, this no-huddle, run-and-shoot-style attack emphasized quick passes, wide formations, and high-tempo play, revolutionizing small-college football by shattering Division 1-AA passing records; and WHEREAS, in 1984, the duo led the Delta Devils to a 9-2 record, averaging 57.1 points per game while Totten threw for 4,557 yards and 56 touchdowns, and Rice caught 112 passes for 1,845 yards and 27 scores; and WHEREAS, this explosive partnership propelled Jerry Rice, widely regarded as the greatest wide receiver in NFL history, onto the national stage and influenced modern spread offenses; and WHEREAS, Mississippi Valley State University (MVSU), a historically Black college and university (HBCU) founded in 1946, joined the Southwestern Athletic Conference (SWAC) in 1968 and competed as the Delta Devils football team.  Prior to the 1980s, the program faced persistent struggles, including multiple losing seasons and a twelve-year drought without a winning record until 1978, marked by records such as 1-8 in 1970 and five-consecutive sub-.500 finishes from 1971 to 1975; and WHEREAS, Archie Cooley became Head Coach at MVSU in 1980.  In 1981, he paired Quarterback Willie Totten with freshman wide receiver Jerry Rice, initiating a quarterback-receiver duo that would define the program's offensive identity; and WHEREAS, Totten, who arrived as a punter and backup signal-caller amid competition from eight other quarterbacks, earned the nickname "The Satellite" for his precise, long-range passes, while Rice was dubbed "World" for his exceptional catching ability, together branding their partnership as the "Satellite Express."  This aerial combination, supported by receivers like Joe Johnson and Carl Byrum, emerged as the offense shifted from traditional ground-based schemes like the veer to experimental pass-heavy strategies; and WHEREAS, as a small Historically Black College and University (HBCU) with enrollment around 3,000 students in the late 1970s and early 1980s, MVSU operated under severe budget constraints that limited recruiting and facilities, necessitating low-cost, innovative tactics like the no-huddle approach to compete in the SWAC without extensive personnel depth.  Cooley's philosophy of rapid, pass-focused play leveraged these limitations, allowing the Satellite Express to thrive by unpacking defensive boxes and tiring opponents through tempo; and WHEREAS, Willie Totten, born on July 4, 1962, in Leflore, Mississippi, grew up in the nearby Town of North Carrollton, where he excelled in football at J.Z. George High School from 1976 to 1981.  As a standout quarterback, Totten drew attention from college programs and enrolled at Mississippi Valley State University (MVSU), joining the Delta Devils football team in 1981.  During his college career from 1981 to 1985 at MVSU, Totten established himself as one of the most prolific passers in Division 1-AA history, accumulating 12,711 passing yards and 141 touchdown passes records that ranked him first in career touchdown passes and among the leaders in passing yards at the time; and WHEREAS, Jerry Rice was born on October 13, 1962, in Starkville, Mississippi, and grew up in nearby Crawford as one of eight children to bricklayer Joe Nathan Rice and his wife Eddie.  An All-State performer at B.L. Moor High School in Oktibbeha County, Rice received limited college recruiting interest due to his small-school background and lack of exposure.  He earned a walk-on opportunity at Mississippi Valley State University (MVSU) in 1980 after impressing coaches during an impromptu tryout, where he showcased his speed and catching ability despite no prior formal invitation; and WHEREAS, over four seasons from 1981 to 1984 at MVSU, Jerry Rice established himself as one of the most prolific receivers in college football history, recording 301 receptions for 4,693 yards and 50 touchdowns.  His yards per catch averaged 15.6, and he set multiple NCAA Division 1-AA records, including 28 touchdown receptions in 1983 and a streak of 13-consecutive games with a touchdown reception spanning 1983-1984. In his senior year of 1984 alone, Rice caught 112 passes for 1,845 yards and 27 touchdowns, shattering his own previous single-season marks and earning him first-team All-American honors from the Associated Press and Football Writers Association of America.  As the centerpiece of MVSU's Satellite Express offense under Coach Archie Cooley, Rice served as the primary target for quarterback Willie Totten, leveraging his exceptional speed, sure hands, and elusiveness after the catch to exploit isolation routes and five wide receiver sets; and WHEREAS, Jerry Rice's collegiate dominance led to his selection by the San Francisco 49ers in the first round (16th overall) of the 1985 NFL Draft, a remarkable rise for a player from a Division 1-AA program.  Despite initial skepticism about his competition level, Rice made an immediate NFL impact as a rookie, recording 49 receptions for 911 yards and three touchdowns while helping the 49ers reach Super Bowl XIX.  The high-volume passing environment at MVSU directly translated to his pro success, instilling the work ethic and route mastery that defined his Hall of Fame career; and WHEREAS, the supporting cast of the Satellite Express offense was instrumental in executing Coach Archie Cooley's high-tempo, pass-oriented scheme, providing the necessary protection, misdirection, and overall team support to sustain the unit's explosive production.  The offensive line, affectionately nicknamed "Tons of Fun" for their size and physical dominance, formed the foundation of the attack, particularly in pass-heavy sets where they prioritized quick protection to allow the quarterback ample time in the pocket; and WHEREAS, MVSU would rename Magnolia Field "Rice-Totten Stadium" in honor of the duo (Totten holds the distinction of being one of the few individuals to coach in a stadium named after him).  Totten was inducted into the College Football Hall of Fame in 2005.  Rice would be inducted into the College Football Hall of Fame in 2006 and the Pro Football Hall of Fame in 2010.  Both are also members of Phi Beta Sigma Fraternity; and WHEREAS, the Satellite Express offense played a pivotal role in popularizing the no-huddle, pass-heavy approach that foreshadowed modern spread and air raid systems in both college and professional football.  In college football, the Satellite Express accelerated the evolution toward pass-oriented offenses, influencing the broader spread of schemes that relied on quick decisions, isolation routes, and numerical advantages against defenses.  At the professional level, the system's validation came through Jerry Rice's transcendent NFL career with the San Francisco 49ers, where his record-setting college production fueled by over 300 receptions in Cooley's scheme translated directly to dominance in Bill Walsh's West Coast offense; and WHEREAS, in recent years, the Satellite Express has experienced renewed interest through podcasts and retrospectives, often revisiting Totten and Rice's 1984 record-setting season. Coverage following Coach Cooley's death in 2024, including obituaries in major outlets, reignited discussions of the offense's cultural footprint, amplifying its status as a foundational element of modern passing schemes.  Rice's enduring NFL fame has further sustained this recognition, with the duo frequently cited in analyses of football history; and WHEREAS, it is with great pride that we remember the legacy and cultural impact of this "dynamic duo" of college football who continue to bring a positive athletic background to the State of Mississippi: NOW, THEREFORE, BE IT RESOLVED BY THE SENATE OF THE STATE OF MISSISSIPPI, THE HOUSE OF REPRESENTATIVES CONCURRING THEREIN, That we do hereby commemorate and remember the legacy and cultural impact of Willie "The Satellite" Totten and Jerry "World" Rice who played for the Mississippi Valley State Delta Devils from 1981-1985 and were nicknamed the "Satellite Express," and extend the best wishes of the Legislature to these Mississippi athletic icons. BE IT FURTHER RESOLVED, That this resolution be presented to Willie "The Satellite" Totten and Jerry "World" Rice, forwarded to the Mississippi Sports Hall of Fame, and made available to the Capitol Press Corps.
